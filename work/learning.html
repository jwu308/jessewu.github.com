Always Learning
Helping MongoDB University students keep track of their course progress.
Problem For each course in MongoDB University, there are syllabus and progress pages. The syllabus page shows a list of the chapter sections that make up the course, and the progress page shows students their current grades in the course.




However, the MongoDB University team had been receiving questions and complaints regarding these pages from users in the community. For example, many users often submitted reports regarding their confusion about:

  • Grading policy
  • Current progress in the course
  • Homework deadlines
  • Countless other issues

I was asked to investigate these usability issues with the syllabus and progress pages and design newly redesigned experiences to address the users' problems. Logistics
  • Project Type: Internship
  • Team: 1 Designer, 1 Product Manager, 1 Developer
  • My Roles: Research, Interaction Design, Data Visualizing, User Testing
  • Time Frame: 5 Weeks
Design Process Research To learn more about how users currently interact with the syllabus and progress pages and empathize with their paint points, needs, and goals when taking a MongoDB University course, two research methods were conducted:

  • Interviews
  • Card Sorting

For the interviews, 11 users were asked a variety of open-ended questions about the context in which they complete MongoDB courses, their goals when taking courses, and issues with interacting with the syllabus and progress pages. These users primarily consisted of data administrators and developers.

For the card sorting activity, key pieces of information about courses, such as chapter titles, chapter start dates, and homework grades, were written on cards. The same 11 users were asked to organize these cards in order of important to them through three phases of taking a course:

  • Browsing a course initially
  • Completing course work during the course
  • Evaluating results after the course has finished

After the user research was conducted and the results were synthesized, the main research insights were:

  • In the beginning of a course, users would like to understand an overview of the course material as well as the time commitment needed to complete the course
  • During the middle of a course, users would like to easily access homework assignments, understand their progress in terms of lessons left to complete, and know their current grades
  • At the end of a course, users would like to understand their performance and access further resources to address areas of weakness and related areas of study
 Ideation and Early Design Based on the user research insights, several primary user tasks were determined:

  • Browse an overview of the course's material and chapter structure
  • Access homework assignments
  • Keep track the amount of completed work and incomplete work
  • Understand their grades
  • Refer to additional resources to improve areas of weakness or learn more about the course

Before jumping into sketching out interface details in wireframes, the overall primary user tasks were determined to understand how the sign-up page would function at a high level. The user tasks were:

  • Sign-up
  • Sign-in
  • Recover password
  • Switch from sign-up to sign-in, or vice versa

To design an experience that enabled users to complete the multitude of user tasks, the tasks were organized into three task categories:

  • Chapter organization
  • Grades
  • Progress of completion

Next, UI designs were ideated via sketching for each of the categories resulting in several components and component layouts.

To determine which UI designs to move forward with, several factors were considered:

  • How well do the UI designs address the user tasks?
  • How well do the designs synergize with the designs of the other task categories?



Based on the design factors mentioned above, the individual UI designs for each task category were weaved together into a coherent wireframe sketch.



The top section shows a description of the course as well an overview of the students' progress and grades.

The user can also see a breakdown of the chapters they're currently working as well as the ones they finished and yet to finish. The ones they are working on are placed towards the top so that users can see them first.

The syllabus and progress pages were also combined into a single page. The rationale behind this design decision was that many of the requested user tasks were similar to one another and could thus accomplished in a unified, hybrid design layout with included elements from both former pages. Peer Review and Design Critique After creating initial wireframes for the design, a peer review and design critique were conducted with internal team members to gather input on the wireframe designs.

For each these evaluation methods, the user tasks were introduced, and explanations were given regarding how the user tasks could be completed through the wireframe design.

The main pieces of feedback were:

  • Given engineering constraint of an additive grading system, in which grades start at 0% and increase towards 100%, seemed discouraging
  • Percentage grades for each chapter does not show weighted grade values; i.e., a chapter with more homework should be weighted more heavily
  • Expanded chapter content would be too extensive in most realistic cases and would thus make the dropdown interfaces very long
 Updated Design Based on the feedback from the internal evaluation sessions, several changes were made to the wireframe design.

The updated design changes that were implemented were:

  • Replaced the overall grade percentage with a data visualization that showed grading category breakdowns, which was homework as 50% and the exam as 50%
  • Replaced the chapter grade percentages with correct and incorrect badges to show the graded weight; i.e.. more badges for a chapter means more impact on the overall grade
  • Removed the accordion dropdown and changed the interaction of clicking on a chapter to navigating to the course page itself, which already shows an outline of the chapter

Next, the mockups were connected together to create an Invision prototype, which can be accessed here.



User Testing After the prototype was created, user testing was conducted to:

  • Determine whether users would be able to complete the user tasks
  • Gather any other input they may have, such as whether the user tasks accurately reflect their needs and goals

The user testing methods used included:

  • Think aloud task walkthrough
  • Follow-up interviews

Users were given a scenario as context and then asked to perform tasks in the prototype based on the scenario.

An example of a task scenario was having the user imagine that they have a busy schedule and then asking them to try to determine the amount of coursework they need to finish by a certain deadline.

After each of these tasks, users were asked follow-up questions about their decision making.

A total of 11 user tests were conducted on external users, who consisted of both database administrators and developers. The results were analyzed, and the main user testing insights were:

  • Users could tell what the course was about, but would still like a brief written description of the course instead of having to look at the chapter titles
  • Users had trouble determining how long the course would last for
  • About half of the users understood the overall grade visualization, while half of the users did not
  • Users liked the badge visualization for graded lessons because they clearly display the outcome of a lesson as well as more contextual information upon hovering
  • Users wanted to see a recommendation of next steps to take after completing a course, such as taking another related course or reading documentation related to the course
 Updated Design Based on the user testing results, several design iterations were made:

  • Added a course description to the top overview section so that users can read a brief summary of the course's content
  • Added course duration information to the overview section so users can determine the time commitment for the course without having to read through each chapter's dates
  • Replaced semi-circle grade visualization with a bar chart, which features clear titles for each bar which represents grading categories, such as homework or exam
  • Unified the tooltip styles under a commonly used tooltip style from the MongoDB design system
  • Added recommendations for similar courses and related readings for after users have completed the course




 Next Steps The updated designs were well-received by the MongoDB University and the MongoDB design team.

However, the designs are currently undergoing further final user testing. The implementation of the final design deliverables are tentatively scheduled to be finished by Q1 2019.
